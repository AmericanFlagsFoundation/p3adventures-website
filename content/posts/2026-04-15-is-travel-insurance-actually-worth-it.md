Every traveler has that moment — you're booking flights, clicking through the final checkout screen, and a little checkbox appears: *Add travel insurance for $68?* And you think, *I'll be fine. I'm always fine.*

Maybe you will be. But let's talk about what happens when you're not — and how to make sure you're actually covered when it counts.

## Why Most Travelers Skip It (And Why That Logic Falls Apart)

The most common reason people skip travel insurance is the same reason they don't floss: nothing bad has happened *yet*. It feels like spending money on a problem you don't have.

But consider this — you're spending $3,000 on a trip to Costa Rica or Cuba or the Caribbean. Travel insurance costs 4–8% of your trip cost. That's $120–$240 to protect a $3,000 investment. You'd insure your car, your phone, your laptop — but not the most expensive vacation you've taken all year?

The math doesn't hold up. The *feeling* just does.

## What Travel Insurance Actually Covers (The Part People Don't Read)

Not all policies are created equal, and this is where most travelers go wrong — they buy *something* without reading what that something actually does.

Here's what to look for:

**Trip cancellation/interruption** — This is the big one. If a family emergency, injury, or illness forces you to cancel or cut a trip short, this reimburses your non-refundable costs. Without it, you eat the full loss.

**Emergency medical** — This is *critical* if you're traveling internationally. Most U.S. health insurance plans offer little to no coverage abroad. A medical evacuation from a remote part of Belize or the Dominican Republic can run $50,000–$100,000+. That's not a typo.

**Baggage loss/delay** — Airlines lose bags. It happens. If your bags are delayed for 24+ hours, a good policy covers essentials. If they're lost entirely, you're reimbursed up to the policy limit.

**Travel delay** — Missed connections, weather delays, airline strikes — these can strand you for days. Delay coverage kicks in after a set threshold (typically 6–12 hours) and covers meals, lodging, and rebooking costs.

**Cancel for any reason (CFAR)** — The premium upgrade. This lets you cancel for literally any reason — cold feet, schedule changes, the destination just doesn't feel right anymore — and typically reimburses 50–75% of your costs. It's pricier, but it's the closest thing to a safety net with no asterisks.

## The Situations Where It Saves You

These aren't hypotheticals. They're stories from real travelers:

- A couple books a Caribbean cruise. One of them breaks an ankle two days before departure. Without trip cancellation coverage, they lose $4,200.
- A solo traveler in Guatemala gets appendicitis. Emergency surgery and a medical flight home costs $22,000. With insurance: $250 out-of-pocket for the deductible.
- A family's bags are rerouted to the wrong country on the first day of a 10-day trip. Delay coverage buys them clothes, toiletries, and dinner while they wait.

These aren't worst-case scenarios — they're just *travel*. Stuff happens. The difference between a disaster and an inconvenience is usually preparation.

## When You Might Be Covered Without Knowing It

Before you buy a standalone policy, check what you already have:

**Your credit card** — Many travel credit cards include trip cancellation, delay, and lost baggage coverage automatically when you book with the card. Read your benefits guide — you might be surprised.

**Your health insurance** — Some plans have limited international coverage. Call your insurer before you leave. "Limited" might mean a single out-of-network claim, not actual evacuation coverage.

**Employer benefits** — Some group health plans extend internationally. Same call-ahead advice applies.

The catch: credit card coverage is usually secondary (it kicks in after other insurance pays) and the limits tend to be lower. It's a decent backup — not a full replacement for a real policy.

## What to Actually Look For When Buying

Shop on a comparison site like InsureMyTrip or Squaremouth. Compare at least three policies. Pay attention to:

- **Medical evacuation limits** — Look for $500K minimum
- **Pre-existing condition coverage** — Many policies cover these if you buy within 14–21 days of your initial trip deposit
- **"Cancel for any reason" availability** — Usually must be added at purchase, not later
- **Adventure activity coverage** — If you're hiking, diving, zip-lining, or anything beyond resort-sitting, confirm it's covered. Some policies exclude "extreme sports" — and their definition of extreme might surprise you.

The cheapest policy isn't always the right one. The right one is the one that actually pays when you need it.

## The Bottom Line

Travel insurance won't make your trip more exciting. It won't improve your flights or upgrade your hotel room. What it does is take the financial catastrophe potential off the table — so you can actually relax once you get there.

For short domestic trips with flexible bookings? Maybe skip it. For international travel, adventure destinations, or any trip with significant non-refundable costs — don't leave home without it.

Travel smart. Cover your bases. Then go have the adventure.
